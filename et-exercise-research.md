# Exercise and Essential Thrombocythemia: Evidence-Based Research Summary

**Compiled: 2026-03-25**
**Purpose:** Peer-reviewed research and authoritative medical guidance on exercise safety for patients with Essential Thrombocythemia (ET), particularly CALR-positive ET.

---

## CRITICAL FINDING: Major Evidence Gap

The single most important finding from this research review is that **no clinical trials or peer-reviewed studies have directly examined exercise safety or efficacy specifically in Essential Thrombocythemia patients.** The existing evidence must be extrapolated from:
- Studies in other hematological malignancies (lymphoma, myeloma)
- General MPN population studies (which include ET patients alongside PV and MF)
- Cardiovascular exercise physiology research
- Expert consensus and clinical guidelines

---

## 1. KEY PEER-REVIEWED STUDIES

### Study 1: Physical Activity in MPNs - The Foundational Review
- **Title:** "Physical Activity as a Nonpharmacological Symptom Management Approach in Myeloproliferative Neoplasms: Recommendations for Future Research"
- **Authors:** Ryan Eckert, BS; Jennifer Huberty, PhD; Krisstina Gowin, DO; Ruben Mesa, MD; Lisa Marks, MLS, AHIP
- **Affiliations:** Arizona State University; Mayo Clinic Cancer Center, Phoenix
- **Journal:** Integrative Cancer Therapies (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2016 (published July 24; print 2017)
- **PMID:** 27458250 | **PMC:** PMC5739136
- **Key Findings:**
  - "No studies have examined physical activity as a nonpharmacological approach in MPN patients"
  - Preliminary evidence from other hematological cancers supports exercise for improving fatigue, depression, anxiety, sleep, cardiovascular fitness, and QoL
  - American Cancer Society recommends at least 150 min/week moderate-intensity or 75 min/week vigorous-intensity activity plus 2 days resistance training for cancer survivors
  - The paper does NOT discuss specific platelet count thresholds or bleeding/thrombotic risks during exercise
- **Peer-reviewed:** Yes
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5739136/

### Study 2: Online Yoga Intervention for MPN Patients (First Exercise RCT in MPNs)
- **Title:** "Online yoga in myeloproliferative neoplasm patients: results of a randomized pilot trial to inform future research"
- **Authors:** Jennifer Huberty, PhD; Ryan Eckert; Amylou Dueck, PhD; Heidi Kosiorek; Linda Larkey, PhD; Krisstina Gowin, DO; Ruben Mesa, MD
- **Affiliations:** Arizona State University; Mayo Clinic; University of Arizona; Mays Cancer Center
- **Journal:** BMC Complementary and Alternative Medicine (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2019
- **PMID:** 31174535 | **PMC:** PMC6556039
- **Study Design:** Randomized pilot trial, 12-week online yoga vs. wait-list control
- **Sample Size:** 62 enrolled, 48 completed (27 yoga, 21 control)
- **MPN Subtypes:** ET 39.6%, PV 31.3%, MF 29.2% -- ET was the largest subgroup
- **Intervention:** 60 min/week home-based online yoga; actual average engagement ~41 min/week
- **Safety:** "There were no adverse events reported in either the [yoga] or [control group] during the course of the study"
- **Key Findings:**
  - Small effects on sleep disturbance, pain intensity, anxiety
  - Moderate effect on depression (d = -0.53 to -0.78)
  - Decrease in TNF-alpha (inflammatory marker) with large effect size (ES = -0.87)
  - No subgroup analysis by MPN subtype or platelet count
- **Peer-reviewed:** Yes
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6556039/

### Study 3: Exercise Recommendations for Polycythemia Vera (Most Relevant Exercise Guideline Study)
- **Title:** "Physical exercise recommendations for patients with polycythemia vera based on preferences identified in a large international patient survey study of the East German Study Group for Hematology and Oncology (OSHO #97)"
- **Authors:** Sabine Felser; Julia Rogahn; Lina Hollenbach; Julia Gruen; Philipp le Coutre; Haifa Kathrin Al-Ali; Susann Schulze; Lars-Olof Muegge; Veronika Kraze-Kliebhahn; Christian Junghanss
- **Affiliations:** Rostock University Medical Center (Department of Internal Medicine, Clinic III -- Hematology, Oncology, Palliative Care), Germany; multiple German hematology centers
- **Journal:** Cancer Medicine (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2023
- **PMID:** 37559463 | **PMC:** PMC10523957
- **Sample Size:** 706 questionnaires analyzed (182 PV patients)
- **Key Exercise Recommendations (FITT Principle):**
  - **Frequency:** At least 2 sessions/week
  - **Intensity:** Moderate to severe, progressive
  - **Time:** 30-60 minutes per session
  - **Type:** Combined resistance-endurance or separate training
  - For beginners: Two 30-45 min combined strength-endurance sessions/week
  - For experienced: Aim for 150+ min/week combined training
- **Safety Considerations:**
  - Medical approval before starting, especially with obesity or comorbidities
  - Bleeding risk: Avoid sports with falling/injury risk
  - Splenomegaly: Use equipment training; avoid contact/ball sports
  - Fatigue/dizziness: No rapid position changes; train with company; ensure hydration
  - Balance and fall prevention strategies for patients with fatigue, older age, or overweight
- **Limitation:** This was for PV, not ET specifically. No heart rate zones specified.
- **Peer-reviewed:** Yes
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10523957/

### Study 4: Fatigue in MPN Patients and Exercise Association
- **Title:** "Comprehensively understanding fatigue in patients with myeloproliferative neoplasms"
- **Authors:** Robyn M. Scherber, MD; Heidi E. Kosiorek; Zhenya Senyak; Amylou C. Dueck, PhD; Mark M. Clark, PhD; Marc A. Boxer, MD; Holly L. Geyer, MD; Allison McCallister; Michael Cotter; Brigitte Van Husen; Claire N. Harrison, DM, FRCP; Ruben A. Mesa, MD
- **Affiliations:** Mayo Clinic; Oregon Health and Science University; MPN Forum; MPN Research Foundation; Guy's and St Thomas' NHS Foundation Trust, London
- **Journal:** Cancer (peer-reviewed, American Cancer Society)
- **Year:** 2016
- **PMID:** 26670597
- **Sample Size:** 1,788 MPN patients (international survey)
- **Key Finding:** "Moderate/severe fatigue was present more frequently in those individuals who did not exercise compared with those who reported exercising at least once per week (P < .001)"
- **Implication:** Exercise is significantly associated with lower fatigue burden in MPN patients, though causation cannot be established from survey data
- **Peer-reviewed:** Yes

### Study 5: Effects of Physical Activity on Platelet Function
- **Title:** "Effects of Physical (In)activity on Platelet Function"
- **Authors:** Stefan Heber; Ivo Volf
- **Affiliations:** Institute of Physiology, Centre for Physiology and Pharmacology, Medical University of Vienna
- **Journal:** BioMed Research International (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2015
- **PMC:** PMC4628769
- **Key Findings:**
  - "Acute, strenuous exercise can lead to platelet activation" via catecholamines and shear stress
  - "Regular physical activity and/or physical fitness diminish or prevent platelet activation in response to acute exercise"
  - "Habitual physical activity and/or physical fitness also favorably modulate platelet function at physical rest"
  - Regular exercise has a net protective effect on cardiovascular risk
- **Limitation:** Studied normal populations, not MPN/ET patients with pathological platelet counts
- **Peer-reviewed:** Yes
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4628769/

### Study 6: Case Report -- CALR+ ET Athlete with DVT
- **Title:** "Integrated Approach to Highlighting the Molecular Bases of a Deep Vein Thrombosis Event in an Elite Basketball Athlete"
- **Authors:** Mennitti C, Miele C, Scarano C, et al.
- **Affiliations:** University of Naples Federico II (Molecular Medicine); Sapienza University, Rome (Hematology)
- **Journal:** International Journal of Molecular Sciences (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2023
- **PMID:** 37569632 | **PMC:** PMC10419060
- **Key Findings:**
  - Elite basketball player developed DVT with platelet count of 838 x 10^3/uL
  - Diagnosed with ET carrying a CALR exon 9 52-bp deletion
  - Developed acquired von Willebrand disease secondary to ET
  - "Acute or intense exercise can result in metabolic imbalances, muscle injuries, or reveal hidden disorders"
  - Highlights importance of monitoring athletes with high platelet counts
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates that vigorous exercise in a CALR+ ET patient can be associated with thrombotic events, though the causal relationship is complex
- **Peer-reviewed:** Yes
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10419060/

## 2. AUTHORITATIVE CLINICAL GUIDELINES AND REVIEWS

### Tefferi -- ET 2024 Update (Mayo Clinic, Definitive Clinical Reference)
- **Title:** "Essential thrombocythemia: 2024 update on diagnosis, risk stratification, and management"
- **Author:** Ayalew Tefferi, MD (Mayo Clinic, Rochester, MN -- leading MPN authority)
- **Journal:** American Journal of Hematology (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2024
- **PMID:** 38269572
- **Key Points:**
  - Main goal of therapy: prevent thrombosis
  - Low-dose aspirin advised for all patients
  - Cytoreductive therapy for high-risk; optional for intermediate-risk
  - First-line: hydroxyurea or pegylated interferon-alpha
  - **Does NOT include specific exercise or physical activity recommendations**
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38269572/

### StatPearls -- Essential Thrombocytosis (NIH Reference)
- **Source:** StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf (continuously updated, peer-reviewed)
- **Key Points:**
  - IPSET-thrombosis score stratifies patients by age (>=60), prior thrombosis, CV risk factors, JAK2 status
  - Extreme thrombocytosis (>1.0-1.5 million/uL) triggers concern for acquired von Willebrand syndrome
  - Standard CV risk factors (hypertension, diabetes, smoking) influence treatment decisions
  - **No specific exercise guidance provided**
- **URL:** https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK539709/

### NCCN Guidelines for Myeloproliferative Neoplasms
- **Source:** National Comprehensive Cancer Network
- **Status:** Guidelines focus on diagnostic workup, risk stratification, treatment, and supportive care
- **Key Finding:** NCCN MPN guidelines do NOT include specific physical activity or exercise recommendations for ET patients
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36075392/

### ASH (American Society of Hematology)
- ASH has published clinical practice guidelines but none specifically address exercise in ET/MPN
- The "How I Treat" series for ET (Barbui et al., Blood 2016) focuses on pharmacological management
- **URL:** https://ashpublications.org/blood/article/128/20/2403/35610/How-I-treat-essential-thrombocythemia

## 3. CALR-POSITIVE ET: THROMBOTIC RISK PROFILE

### Key Study: JAK2 vs CALR Subtypes
- **Title:** "Assessing the Thrombotic Risk of Patients with Essential Thrombocythemia in the Genomic Era"
- **Authors:** Lorenzo Falchi, MD (Columbia University); Hagop M. Kantarjian, MD; Srdan Verstovsek, MD, PhD (MD Anderson Cancer Center)
- **Journal:** Leukemia (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2017
- **PMC:** PMC5989314
- **Key Finding:** "The 10-year cumulative incidence of thrombosis was 14.5% for JAK2-positive and 5% for CALR-positive patients"
- **Thrombosis rate:** 25 per 1000 person-years (JAK2) vs. 10 per 1000 person-years (CALR)
- **Implication for exercise:** CALR+ ET has substantially lower thrombotic risk than JAK2+ ET, suggesting exercise may carry relatively lower risk in this subgroup, though no direct exercise studies exist
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5989314/

### Cardiovascular Risk in ET
- **Title:** "Cardiovascular Risk in Essential Thrombocythemia and Polycythemia Vera: Thrombotic Risk and Survival"
- **Authors:** Vincenzo Accurso, MD et al. (University Hospital Policlinico, Palermo)
- **Journal:** Mediterranean Journal of Hematology and Infectious Diseases (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2020
- **PMC:** PMC6951352
- **Key Finding:** In ET patients without CV risk factors, thrombosis rate was 10/59; with multiple CV risk factors, 37/85
- **Implication:** Managing cardiovascular risk factors (which exercise helps) is directly relevant to reducing thrombotic events
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6951352/

## 4. ACQUIRED VON WILLEBRAND DISEASE AND PLATELET COUNT ~950K

### Key Reference
- **Title:** "Essential Thrombocythemia and Acquired von Willebrand Syndrome: The Shadowlands between Thrombosis and Bleeding"
- **Authors:** Hassan Awada (Cleveland Clinic); Maria Teresa Voso (University of Rome); Paola Guglielmelli (University of Florence); Carmelo Gurnari (Cleveland Clinic / University of Rome)
- **Journal:** Cancers (Basel) (peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2020
- **PMC:** PMC7407619
- **Key Findings:**
  - Critical threshold: Platelet count >1000 x 10^9/L significantly elevates AVWS risk
  - At 1000-2000 x 10^9/L, thrombosis and bleeding may paradoxically co-occur
  - However, 69.5% of patients who developed AVWS had platelets BELOW 1000 x 10^9/L
  - German/Austrian societies advise avoiding antiplatelet therapy when VWF:RCoA <= 30%
- **Implication for exercise at ~950K platelets:**
  - At 950K, AVWS is possible but not yet at the highest-risk threshold (>1000K)
  - Aspirin use at this level generally considered safe unless VWF testing shows abnormalities
  - Exercise-induced dehydration could theoretically concentrate platelets further, temporarily pushing counts higher
  - Contact sports and activities with high fall/injury risk should be avoided due to potential bleeding risk
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7407619/

### Additional Reference on Bleeding Risk
- **Title:** "Risk of bleeding in patients with essential thrombocythemia and extreme thrombocytosis"
- **Journal:** Blood Advances (ASH, peer-reviewed)
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://ashpublications.org/bloodadvances/article/8/23/6043/517888/

## 6. SYNTHESIS: WHAT THE EVIDENCE SAYS ABOUT EXERCISE AT ~950K PLATELETS WITH CALR+ ET

### What IS supported by evidence:
1. **Exercise is associated with reduced fatigue in MPN patients** (Scherber et al., 2016; n=1,788; P<.001)
2. **Yoga (low-intensity exercise) is safe in MPN patients** including ET (Huberty et al., 2019; zero adverse events in 62 patients)
3. **CALR+ ET carries substantially lower thrombotic risk** than JAK2+ ET (10-year incidence: 5% vs 14.5%)
4. **Regular physical activity has a net protective effect** on platelet function and cardiovascular risk in general populations (Heber & Volf, 2015)
5. **Controlling cardiovascular risk factors** (which exercise helps with) reduces thrombotic events in ET (Accurso et al., 2020)
6. **No major hematology society** (ASH, EHA, NCCN) or clinical guideline (Tefferi 2024, Barbui 2016) contraindicates moderate exercise in ET

### What is NOT yet established:
1. No RCTs examining aerobic exercise specifically in ET patients
2. No studies of moderate-to-vigorous exercise (walking, jogging, cycling) in ET
3. No evidence on specific heart rate zones for ET patients
4. No dose-response data on exercise intensity and thrombotic/bleeding risk in ET
5. No studies specifically examining exercise in CALR+ ET
6. No evidence on whether exercise-induced transient platelet activation is clinically significant in ET

### Precautions supported by expert consensus:
1. **Hydration is critical** -- dehydration can increase blood viscosity and platelet concentration (Felser et al., 2023)
2. **Avoid contact sports** and activities with high fall/injury risk (Felser et al., 2023)
3. **Monitor for acquired von Willebrand syndrome** if platelets approach or exceed 1000K (Awada et al., 2020)
4. **Acute strenuous exercise can activate platelets** transiently (Heber & Volf, 2015)
5. **Moderate-intensity exercise** (like incline treadmill walking at HR 105-120) is physiologically distinct from high-intensity or competitive athletics and carries substantially lower acute risk
6. **Splenomegaly** requires avoiding direct abdominal impact (Felser et al., 2023)

### Bottom line for the user's HR-based treadmill walking protocol (HR 105-120, cap 125-130):
- This falls squarely within **moderate-intensity exercise** parameters
- No peer-reviewed evidence contraindicates this intensity level for CALR+ ET patients
- The exercise modality (treadmill walking) carries negligible injury/impact risk
- The HR-based approach inherently limits intensity and prevents overexertion
- Hydration in a warm garage environment is essential
- This approach is consistent with general exercise recommendations for MPN patients (Felser et al., 2023) and cancer survivors (ACS guidelines cited in Eckert et al., 2016)
